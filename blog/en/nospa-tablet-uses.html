Nospa Tablet Uses, Dosage and Side Effects in Pakistan

TL;DR — Key Takeaways
  • Nospa = drotaverine, an antispasmodic smooth muscle relaxant
  • Relieves stomach cramps, period pain, biliary colic, renal colic
  • Works within 30–60 minutes; takes the "grip" out of spasmodic pain
  • Does NOT treat the underlying cause — used for symptomatic relief
  • Generally very safe; mild side effects

What Is Nospa?

Nospa (No-Spa) is the brand name for drotaverine hydrochloride, an antispasmodic that relaxes smooth muscle by inhibiting PDE4 (phosphodiesterase 4). Unlike NSAIDs (Brufen, Ponstan) that work on pain signals, Nospa works directly on the muscle itself — relaxing spasm and relieving colic-type pain. It is widely used across Pakistan, Eastern Europe, and Asia for stomach cramps, period pain, and biliary or renal colic.

Nospa Tablet Uses

1. Abdominal Cramps and Stomach Pain

The most common use; effective for smooth muscle spasm anywhere in the GI tract.

2. Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)

Relieves the cramping and spasmodic pain of IBS.

3. Biliary Colic (Gallbladder Cramps)

Relaxes the bile duct to relieve the pain of gallbladder spasm or passing a small stone.

4. Renal Colic (Kidney Stone Pain)

Relaxes the ureter, helping small stones pass and reducing ureteral spasm pain.

5. Menstrual Cramps (Dysmenorrhea)

An effective alternative to NSAIDs for period pain, especially when stomach sensitivity is a concern.

6. Peptic Ulcer-Related Spasm

Adjunct to anti-ulcer therapy for cramping.

7. Labour Pain (Obstetric Use)

Used in some settings to aid cervical dilation during labour.

8. Urinary Tract Spasm

After urological procedures or with UTI causing bladder spasm.

How Nospa Differs from Pain Killers (NSAIDs)

Nospa is NOT a painkiller in the traditional sense — it does not block pain signals. It relaxes the muscle causing the pain. This makes it:

  • Better than NSAIDs for spasmodic/colic pain where the muscle itself is the problem
  • Safer for stomach — does not cause GI ulcers like NSAIDs
  • Ideal for pregnancy — no prostaglandin effects (though always consult doctor)
  • Safe for dengue — no effect on platelets unlike NSAIDs

However, it does NOT work for headaches, muscular injuries, fever, or non-spasmodic pain.

Side Effects

Common:

  • Headache
  • Dizziness
  • Nausea
  • Heart palpitations (mild)
  • Hypotension (low blood pressure — especially with first dose)
  • Dry mouth (mild)

Serious (rare):

  • Severe allergic reaction
  • Significant hypotension in susceptible patients

Who Should NOT Take Nospa

  • Severe kidney failure
  • Severe liver failure
  • Severe heart failure with low blood pressure
  • Hypersensitivity to drotaverine
  • Angle-closure glaucoma (relative contraindication)
  • Not recommended for children under 6 years (consult paediatrician)

Important: When Nospa Is Not Enough

Nospa relieves the cramp/spasm but does not treat the underlying cause. See a doctor if:

  • Pain is severe and not relieved within 1–2 hours
  • Pain is accompanied by fever (could indicate appendicitis, cholecystitis, pyelonephritis)
  • Vomiting blood or passing black stools
  • Severe flank pain — could be a large kidney stone requiring urological intervention
  • Chest pain radiating to back — could be aortic dissection, not GI

Do not mask serious pain with Nospa and delay seeking medical care.

Nospa in Pregnancy

Nospa (drotaverine) does not have prostaglandin-inhibiting effects, making it theoretically safer than NSAIDs in early pregnancy for cramping. It has been used in obstetrics in some countries for cervical dilation. However, always consult your doctor before taking any medication in pregnancy. Some guidelines advise caution in the first trimester.

Nospa vs Buscopan vs Ponstan — Which for Period Pain?

  • Nospa (drotaverine): Relaxes uterine smooth muscle — good first-line
  • Buscopan (hyoscine): Another antispasmodic — also effective, slightly drier effects
  • Ponstan (mefenamic acid): NSAID that reduces prostaglandins — often stronger for severe dysmenorrhea but harder on stomach
  • Brufen (ibuprofen): NSAID — also effective but GI risk

Many gynaecologists combine Nospa + Ponstan for severe period cramps.

Conclusion

Nospa (drotaverine) is one of Pakistan's most trusted and safest medicines for stomach cramps, period pain, biliary colic, and IBS. It works by relaxing the smooth muscle in spasm — not by masking pain signals. Side effects are mild. It is generally safe in dengue (unlike NSAIDs) and has a better stomach profile. However, never ignore severe or persistent abdominal pain — use Nospa for known spasmodic conditions and seek a diagnosis for new or severe symptoms.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Nospa tablet used for?

Nospa (drotaverine 40 mg) is used for stomach cramps, IBS (irritable bowel syndrome), biliary colic (gallbladder cramps), renal colic (kidney stone pain), menstrual cramps (period pain), and urinary tract spasm. It works by relaxing smooth muscle that is in spasm — providing relief from the cramping, colicky pain.

How quickly does Nospa work?

Nospa typically starts working within 30–60 minutes of taking a tablet. For acute colic pain, 80 mg (2 tablets) can be taken immediately. For period cramps, it works best when taken at the first sign of cramping rather than waiting for the pain to become severe.

Can Nospa be taken during pregnancy?

Nospa (drotaverine) is considered safer than NSAIDs during pregnancy since it does not inhibit prostaglandins. It has been used in obstetrics in some countries. However, always consult your doctor before taking any medication in pregnancy — even medications considered relatively safe.

Is Nospa safe during dengue fever?

Yes — Nospa is generally considered safe during dengue fever, unlike NSAIDs (Brufen, Ponstan, Synflex) which are contraindicated due to bleeding risk. Nospa does not affect platelets or clotting. However, for dengue fever itself, paracetamol remains the recommended treatment.

Can I take Nospa and Ponstan together for period pain?

Yes — this combination is sometimes recommended by gynaecologists for severe dysmenorrhea. Nospa relaxes the uterine smooth muscle in spasm, while Ponstan (mefenamic acid) reduces prostaglandins that cause the cramps. However, adding Ponstan brings NSAID side effects (GI risk), so take both with food and avoid if you have a peptic ulcer.
