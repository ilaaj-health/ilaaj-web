Buscopan Tablet Uses, Dosage and Side Effects in Pakistan

TL;DR — Key Takeaways
  • Buscopan = hyoscine butylbromide 10 mg, an anticholinergic antispasmodic that does NOT cause sedation
  • Relieves stomach cramps, IBS, biliary colic, renal colic, period pain and bladder spasms
  • Standard adult dose: 10–20 mg (1–2 tablets) three to four times daily; max 80 mg/day
  • Avoid if you have glaucoma, BPH, myasthenia gravis or a fast heart rate
  • Broader than Nospa — covers bladder spasms too — but has more anticholinergic side effects

What Is Buscopan?

Buscopan is the brand name for hyoscine butylbromide (also called scopolamine butylbromide), a quaternary ammonium anticholinergic antispasmodic manufactured by Sanofi and widely available across Pakistan. Each tablet contains 10 mg of the active ingredient. Unlike the scopolamine patches used for motion sickness, hyoscine butylbromide does not cross the blood-brain barrier — meaning it has no sedating, hallucinogenic or central nervous system effects. It works entirely in the periphery, targeting the smooth muscle of the gut, biliary tract, urinary tract and uterus.

Buscopan has been in clinical use for over 60 years and remains one of the most prescribed antispasmodics in Pakistan for conditions ranging from irritable bowel syndrome to biliary colic and dysmenorrhea.

How Does Buscopan Work?

Buscopan blocks muscarinic M1 and M3 receptors in the smooth muscle of the gastrointestinal tract, biliary system, urinary tract and uterus. Normally, the neurotransmitter acetylcholine binds to these receptors and stimulates muscle contraction. In conditions like IBS, biliary colic or renal colic, the smooth muscle goes into excessive, painful spasm. By blocking acetylcholine's binding site, Buscopan directly relaxes this spasm within 15–30 minutes of oral administration.

Because hyoscine butylbromide is a quaternary ammonium compound — positively charged and poorly lipid-soluble — it is unable to cross the lipid-rich blood-brain barrier. This is what separates it from the older antimuscarinics like atropine or hyoscine hydrobromide (the patch form), which do enter the brain and cause sedation and cognitive side effects.

Buscopan Tablet Uses

1. Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)

IBS is one of the most common gastrointestinal conditions in Pakistan, causing recurring cramping, bloating and altered bowel habits. The cramping in IBS is caused by exaggerated smooth muscle contraction in the colon. Buscopan is recommended in international and local guidelines as a first-line antispasmodic for IBS-related abdominal pain. It reduces the intensity and frequency of spasms, making it particularly useful during flare-ups. Many Pakistani gastroenterologists prescribe Buscopan as part of IBS management alongside dietary modification and, if needed, low-dose antidepressants.

2. Stomach and Intestinal Cramps

Any cause of smooth muscle spasm in the GI tract — including gastroenteritis, food intolerance, post-meal cramping, or spasm related to constipation — can respond to Buscopan. It is the go-to antispasmodic when the pain has a classic "gripping" or "wave-like" colicky character, which indicates smooth muscle in spasm rather than an inflammatory or structural cause.

3. Biliary Colic (Gallbladder Cramps)

When a gallstone partially obstructs the cystic duct or common bile duct, the gallbladder and bile duct walls go into painful spasm. This produces the classic biliary colic: severe, episodic pain in the right upper abdomen or epigastrium, often radiating to the right shoulder or back. Buscopan relaxes the smooth muscle of the biliary tract, providing meaningful relief during acute episodes. It is often combined with a painkiller (paracetamol or a low-dose NSAID) for biliary colic in Pakistani emergency and outpatient settings.

4. Renal Colic (Kidney Stone Pain)

As a kidney stone moves down the ureter, the ureter wall goes into intense spasm around the stone, producing excruciating flank pain that radiates to the groin — renal colic. Buscopan relaxes ureteral smooth muscle, reducing the intensity of spasm and in some cases helping smaller stones pass more easily. In hospital settings, the injectable form of hyoscine butylbromide is used for acute renal colic alongside NSAIDs or opioids. Oral Buscopan tablets are used for milder or resolving cases.

5. Dysmenorrhea (Period Cramps)

Menstrual cramps are caused by prostaglandin-driven uterine smooth muscle contractions. Buscopan relieves the spasm component of dysmenorrhea — the "gripping" or "twisting" quality of the pain. It works best when taken at the first sign of cramping rather than waiting for peak pain. For severe dysmenorrhea, Buscopan is sometimes combined with mefenamic acid (Ponstan), which additionally reduces prostaglandin production. For women who cannot tolerate NSAIDs due to gastric sensitivity, Buscopan alone or with paracetamol is a gentler alternative.

6. Bladder Spasms

This is where Buscopan has an advantage over Nospa (drotaverine): Buscopan's anticholinergic mechanism extends effectively to the bladder detrusor muscle. It is used for overactive bladder symptoms, bladder spasm after urological procedures, and painful bladder spasm associated with urinary tract infections. Drotaverine (Nospa) does not share this bladder-targeting efficacy as reliably.

7. Pre-procedure and Labour Use (Obstetric)

Buscopan injection is used in some obstetric protocols to relax the cervix and aid cervical dilation during labour. It is administered parenterally in this context, not as a tablet. Some gynaecologists in Pakistan also use it as an antispasmodic during gynaecological examinations or minor procedures to reduce uterine cramping.

8. Peptic Ulcer-Related Spasm

As an adjunct in peptic ulcer disease when cramping or spasm accompanies the ulcer pain, Buscopan can provide symptomatic relief alongside the primary anti-ulcer medications (PPIs, H2 blockers, antibiotics for H. pylori).

Buscopan Side Effects

Because Buscopan is an anticholinergic drug, its side effects follow the classic "anticholinergic toxidrome" pattern — though at standard doses these are usually mild:

Common Side Effects:

  • Dry mouth — the most frequent; caused by reduced salivary gland secretion
  • Blurred vision / difficulty focusing — due to inhibition of ciliary muscle and pupillary dilation
  • Difficulty urinating — especially in men with enlarged prostate (BPH); can cause acute urinary retention
  • Constipation — by reducing intestinal motility (ironic when treating GI cramps)
  • Tachycardia (fast heart rate) — more common at higher doses; inhibition of cardiac muscarinic receptors
  • Flushing and warm skin — due to inhibition of sweat glands

Less Common / Serious:

  • Acute angle-closure glaucoma attack — if the drug is used in a patient with narrow-angle glaucoma; this is a medical emergency (eye pain, vision loss, nausea)
  • Acute urinary retention — in men with significant BPH
  • Severe allergic reaction — rare but possible; stop immediately and seek emergency care if hives, swelling, or breathing difficulty occur
  • Significant tachycardia at high doses

Who Should NOT Take Buscopan

  • Narrow-angle (angle-closure) glaucoma — anticholinergics raise intraocular pressure and can trigger an acute attack
  • Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) / enlarged prostate — risk of acute urinary retention
  • Myasthenia gravis — anticholinergics worsen neuromuscular junction dysfunction
  • Tachyarrhythmias / fast heart rate — Buscopan can further increase heart rate
  • Megacolon / paralytic ileus — reducing gut motility is dangerous
  • Hypersensitivity to hyoscine butylbromide
  • Children under 6 years without paediatric medical advice

Buscopan During Pregnancy

Buscopan injection is used in obstetric labour protocols in Pakistan and internationally to facilitate cervical dilation. However, for general use during pregnancy — especially the first trimester — Buscopan should only be taken on medical advice. Systemic anticholinergic effects can theoretically affect the fetus. If you are pregnant and experiencing cramping, consult your gynaecologist before self-medicating. Do not assume that because it is used in labour it is safe at all stages of pregnancy.

Buscopan with Other Medicines

Safe to combine:

  • Paracetamol (Panadol) — commonly combined for IBS and period pain; paracetamol adds pain relief while Buscopan removes the spasm
  • PPIs (Nexium, Risek, Omeprazole) — safe together for peptic ulcer-associated spasm
  • Antiemetics (Maxolon, Stugeron) — safe if nausea accompanies cramping

Use with caution / avoid:

  • Other anticholinergic drugs (some antihistamines, antidepressants, bladder medications) — additive anticholinergic effects: severe dry mouth, confusion, tachycardia
  • Metoclopramide (Maxolon) — these drugs have opposing effects on gut motility; generally not combined
  • Tricyclic antidepressants — both have anticholinergic properties; risk of additive side effects

When Buscopan Is Not Enough — Red Flags

Buscopan relieves the spasm but does not treat the underlying cause. Never rely solely on Buscopan and delay medical care if you notice any of these warning signs:

  • Pain is severe and not relieved within 1–2 hours
  • Pain accompanied by fever — think appendicitis, cholecystitis, pyelonephritis
  • Vomiting blood or black tarry stools — GI bleeding; emergency
  • Severe right upper quadrant pain with jaundice — obstructing gallstone or cholangitis
  • Severe flank pain with inability to pass urine — large obstructing kidney stone
  • Chest pain radiating to the back — could be aortic dissection, not a GI condition
  • New abdominal pain in elderly or diabetic patients — atypical presentations of serious conditions are common

Buscopan masks pain temporarily — do not use it to suppress symptoms you should be investigating.

Conclusion

Buscopan (hyoscine butylbromide 10 mg) is one of the most effective and well-established antispasmodics available in Pakistan. It works by blocking muscarinic receptors in the smooth muscle of the gut, biliary tract, urinary tract and uterus — providing rapid relief from IBS, biliary colic, renal colic, menstrual cramps and bladder spasms. Its key advantage is breadth of action: it covers bladder spasms that Nospa (drotaverine) may not address as effectively. Its key limitation is a classic anticholinergic side-effect profile — dry mouth, blurred vision, urinary difficulty — and absolute contraindications in narrow-angle glaucoma and significant BPH.

Used correctly, at the right dose, and in the right patient, Buscopan is safe and highly effective. If you are unsure whether your symptoms warrant Buscopan or whether another drug would suit you better, consult a doctor before self-medicating.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Buscopan used for?

Buscopan (hyoscine butylbromide 10 mg) is used for stomach and intestinal cramps, IBS (irritable bowel syndrome), biliary colic from gallstones, renal colic from kidney stones, menstrual cramps (dysmenorrhea), bladder spasms, and to help cervical dilation in labour. It works by blocking muscarinic receptors and relaxing the smooth muscle of the gastrointestinal and urinary tracts.

How quickly does Buscopan work?

Buscopan tablets typically begin working within 15–30 minutes. The injectable form (used in hospitals) works within minutes. For faster onset from the tablet, some doctors recommend letting it dissolve under the tongue, though the standard route is swallowing with water.

Can I take Buscopan during pregnancy?

Buscopan injection is used during labour in some obstetric protocols to aid cervical dilation. However, it should not be used routinely during pregnancy without medical advice. Consult your doctor — especially in the first trimester when drug exposure risk is highest.

What is the difference between Buscopan and Nospa?

Both are antispasmodics but work differently. Buscopan (hyoscine butylbromide) is an anticholinergic that blocks muscarinic receptors — it also affects the bladder and can cause dry mouth, blurred vision, difficulty urinating. Nospa (drotaverine) is a PDE4 inhibitor that selectively relaxes smooth muscle with fewer anticholinergic effects. Nospa is generally preferred in pregnancy and dengue. Buscopan is preferred when bladder spasms are also present.

Can I take Buscopan with Panadol?

Yes — Buscopan and paracetamol (Panadol) can be taken together safely. Buscopan addresses the spasm/cramp component while paracetamol treats the pain. This combination is commonly used for IBS and menstrual cramps. Always use both at the correct dose and avoid exceeding 80 mg/day of Buscopan or 4 g/day of paracetamol.
